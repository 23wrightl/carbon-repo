Can you make a difference?
Afton Turner
Published 11/30/2021

We all take pleasant vacations to the beach or the mountains, but have you ever considered how the road trip or short flight there is hurting the environment?

 Travel is a necessary part of daily life. No matter where you live, you travel somewhere every day. Whether it be driving to work or taking a flight to Colorado, everyone travels.
 Next time you hop in your car or take the bus, consider what that vehicle is doing to the air around us. Or what it's already done to the Earth before you got it.

Winding Roads and Ashy Air
 According to epa.gov, the average US car emits 404 grams of tailpipe CO2 per mile. Per mile! Now take that 404 grams and multiple by the 40 miles an average US citizen can travel in a day. We will do the math for you: 16,160 grams of CO2 per person per day. That is insane! Let’s do this one more time. Take the 404 and multiply it by the average travel distance per day in the US, 11 million miles. That is around 4,444,000,000 grams (4,898.67 tons) of CO2 a day from US travel alone. A year? 1,788,015.09 tons. But that is just tailpipe CO2.
 Let’s talk about production.



Picture from ABC News

 Gasoline-powered cars are environment wreckers. Not only are their tailpipe emissions high, but the production emissions from making them are astonishing; from getting the materials to transporting the vehicles to moving the fuel, the CO2 emissions skyrocket.
 Electric and hybrid vehicles also emit a lot of carbon dioxide when they are produced. They may not emit any tailpipe CO2, but their production and waste are still huge factors. Battery production and disposal contribute to not only carbon emissions but also polluting the land through landfills. It also takes a lot of energy to transport the electricity and parts needed for an electric or hybrid vehicle.

 So, when it comes down to it, the only way to truly be eco-friendly is not to drive, buy a car, or even get inside a vehicle. But that is entirely unrealistic.
 Instead, we can try our best to lead fuel-efficient lives, donate or recycle old cars and batteries when they no longer work, and take heed of our automobile emissions.
 Always remember that a little effort can go a long way.

Taking Flight: The Good, Bad, and Ugly
Domestic flights emit around 133 grams of CO2 on average. According to The International Air Transport Association, or the IATA, air travel was the source of 2% of artificial CO2 emissions in 2017 alone. A round trip from New York to San Francisco, California emits almost one metric ton per flight passenger. The numbers only get higher the further the destination.

 Flying has been around for ages. No one expects to stop flying anytime soon. Well, we don't expect that either.
 The flight industry is a necessary part of travel, and the only reason it has affected the environment is that some people don't have any other choice but to hop on a plane to get where they're going. We are all limited by time; everyone has deadlines, time constraints, due dates, so on and so forth. Most people can't afford the time it takes to drive the 33.5 hours from Salt Lake City, Utah, to Orlando, Florida. But many more can afford to fly the 4 hours it takes to travel the same distance via plane.
 We may not all be capable of engineering a new plane or donating millions of dollars to our favorite airlines, but some of us can still do something. Employees who usually travel for business can make the most prominent difference right now.
 Zoom Video Conferencing became popular during the COVID-19 quarantine in 2020, and many many people still use it to work from home today. This virtual chat software helped eliminate the need for long business-related flights across the country. Now that airlines are opening back up, and more people can fit on planes, some businesses are going back to flying their employees to meetings instead of "Zoom-ing" them.
 If you are an employee and your company is making you fly everywhere, consider speaking up and suggesting a video conferencing software such as Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Skype, Webex Meetings, BlueJeans Meetings, GoToMeeting, Google Workspace, or Jabber.
